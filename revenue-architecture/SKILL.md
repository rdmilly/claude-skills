# Revenue Architecture

A repeatable methodology for converting accumulated technical capability into income. Designed for the builder who has spent months (or years) creating infrastructure, tools, and systems but hasn't yet generated revenue from them.

Extracted from a real planning session where 18+ months of infrastructure building (5 nodes, 47 MCP servers, 730 tools, multiple partially-built products) was mapped to a 90-day revenue plan.

## When to use this skill

- The user has built significant technical infrastructure but isn't generating revenue from it
- The user asks "how do we monetize this" or "what's the business model"
- The user has multiple partially-built projects and needs to prioritize by revenue proximity
- The user wants to transition from building to earning
- The user needs to identify which capabilities serve multiple revenue streams

## The six-step process

### Step 1: Asset inventory with revenue proximity scoring

List EVERYTHING that's been built — not just the flagship projects, but also the infrastructure, tools, data, skills, relationships, and brand assets. Then score each by proximity to revenue using three tiers:

**Tier 1 — Revenue-ready (needs activation, not building):** These assets could generate money within 2-4 weeks if someone just turned them on. Common examples: a lead list that's never been contacted, a service that's fully deliverable but has no clients, an existing business that could upsell.

**Tier 2 — Near-revenue (needs 1-2 weeks of finishing work):** 80%+ built but missing the last mile: packaging, pricing, payment integration, listing, or launch. The work remaining is clear and bounded.

**Tier 3 — Product potential (needs significant build + go-to-market):** The vision is strong and the foundation exists, but months of work remain before anyone would pay for it. These are the long bets.

**Process:**
- List every asset: code, infrastructure, data, brands, domains, existing clients, relationships, content, skills, partially-built products
- For each asset, answer: "If I had to make $1 from this in 14 days, could I? What would I need?"
- Sort into tiers based on the answer
- Be brutally honest: most assets are Tier 3. The value of this step is finding the Tier 1 items you're overlooking because you're excited about Tier 3.

**Failure mode:** Treating everything as Tier 2 because you want it to be closer to revenue than it actually is. Also: ignoring non-technical assets (existing client relationships, brand reputation, domain expertise) that might be the fastest path.

### Step 2: Revenue model research (external landscape)

Search for how other people with similar assets are actually making money. Don't invent models — find proven ones and adapt.

**Process:**
- Search for revenue benchmarks in the user's specific domain ("AI automation agency revenue 2026", "Chrome extension monetization", etc.)
- Find 3-5 documented success stories with real numbers (monthly revenue, margins, team size)
- Extract the common patterns: pricing, delivery model, customer acquisition, retention
- Map each proven model against the user's existing assets: "Which of these could I do with what I already have?"

**Key metrics to find:**
- Revenue per client (for service businesses)
- Conversion rate from free to paid (for products)
- Customer acquisition cost
- Gross margin
- Time to first revenue

**Failure mode:** Getting excited about billion-dollar potential (the HeadshotPro story, the one-person unicorn prediction) when the immediate need is $5,000/month to cover expenses. Match ambition to timeline: what gets you to survival revenue FIRST?

### Step 3: Multi-stream capability mapping

The most important step. Many builders have capabilities that serve multiple revenue streams. Building a landing page skill helps the agency business AND the product business AND the content business. Identifying these shared capabilities means you build one thing and unlock three revenue paths.

**Process:**
- Create a matrix: capabilities on one axis, revenue streams on the other
- For each intersection, mark whether the capability serves that stream directly (✅), indirectly (partial), or not at all
- Identify capabilities that serve 3+ streams — these are the highest-leverage things to build
- Identify revenue streams that need zero new capabilities — these are the fastest to activate

**Output:** A prioritized build list ordered by: (1) how many streams it serves, (2) how close those streams are to revenue, (3) how long it takes to build.

**Failure mode:** Building the most exciting capability instead of the most leveraged one. A capability that serves one Tier 3 stream is less valuable than one that serves two Tier 1 streams, even if the Tier 3 vision is cooler.

### Step 4: Revenue math (conservative, not optimistic)

For each viable revenue stream, build the math. Use conservative numbers — the purpose is to know the floor, not the ceiling.

**Process:**
- Service businesses: (number of clients you can realistically serve) × (average monthly retainer) × (margin after costs)
- Products: (realistic install/signup count in 90 days) × (conversion rate to paid, typically 1-3%) × (monthly price)
- Content: (realistic audience size in 90 days) × (revenue per 1000 views/followers, typically very low)
- Always subtract: hosting costs, tool subscriptions, tax, time cost

**Reality checks:**
- Service revenue is fastest but trades time for money
- Product revenue is slowest to start but compounds without your time
- Content revenue is the slowest of all and requires sustained consistency
- The optimal portfolio has service revenue (immediate) funding product development (future)

**Failure mode:** Using optimistic conversion rates or audience growth numbers. If your plan requires 10,000 users in month 1 to work, it's not a plan — it's a fantasy. Build the plan that works at 100 users.

### Step 5: Sequence the 90-day plan

Map the first 90 days in 2-week blocks. Each block has a clear goal, specific actions, and a revenue target. The sequence follows the proximity tiers: activate Tier 1 first, finish Tier 2 second, build Tier 3 in background.

**Process:**
- Weeks 1-2: Activate ALL Tier 1 assets. Define service packages. Contact leads. Set up payment.
- Weeks 3-4: Close first deals. Ship Tier 2 finishing work (CWS listing, landing page, etc.)
- Weeks 5-8: Deliver to first clients. Start Tier 3 build work during non-client hours.
- Weeks 9-12: Scale service delivery. Launch product betas. Measure and adjust.

**For each 2-week block, specify:**
- What gets built or activated
- Revenue target (even if $0 for early blocks)
- Hours required from the human (not everything is automated)
- Dependencies (what must happen before this block can start)

**Failure mode:** Spending all of weeks 1-4 on Tier 3 product work because it's more fun than sales calls. The plan only works if Tier 1 activation happens FIRST — it funds everything else.

### Step 6: Define the questions the human must answer

The revenue plan always has gaps that require human judgment, not research. Identify these explicitly and present them as decision points, not open-ended brainstorms.

**Common questions:**
- How many hours/week can you dedicate to client delivery vs. product building?
- What's the minimum monthly revenue that changes your life situation? (e.g., drop part-time work, cover all expenses)
- Which market do you want to serve — local (Yamhill County) or remote (anywhere)?
- Are you comfortable with cold outreach (email/phone) or only warm leads (referrals/content)?
- Which projects are passion projects (don't need to make money) vs. business projects (must make money)?

**These questions are blockers.** The plan can't be executed without answers. Present them as a short, numbered list — not buried in prose.

## Output format

After running all six steps, produce:

1. **Asset inventory** — every asset sorted into Tier 1/2/3 with specific gaps identified
2. **Revenue landscape** — 3-5 proven models with real benchmarks, mapped to user's assets
3. **Capability matrix** — which capabilities serve which streams, ordered by leverage
4. **Revenue math** — conservative projections per stream at 3/6/12 month marks
5. **90-day plan** — 2-week blocks with specific actions, targets, and dependencies
6. **Decision points** — numbered list of questions the human must answer before execution

This should feel like a business plan, not a brainstorm. Every number should be defensible. Every action should be specific enough to execute tomorrow.
